ASPIRE: Accessible Surgical-robotics Platform for REsearch in Surgical Training, Skill Evaluation and Autonomy

Sanjay Jayalakshmi Prabakar, Manojkumar Srinivasan, Pranali Ingole, Niravkumar A. Patel
INSPIRE Lab, Indian Institute of Technology Madras, India.
Submitted to RA-L and 2024
Paper Supplementary Code arXiv

Abstract

Minimally invasive robotic surgery (MIRS) systems such as Raven from the University of Washington and the da Vinci Research Kit (dVRK) are primarily used to research surgical training, skill evaluation, and autonomy. However, they require dedicated hardware and software components, which may not be as accessible as serial manipulators such as Universal Robot (UR) 5e or KUKA LBR iiwa7. While many research labs have these serial manipulators, they are often not used for surgical robotics research due to the lack of a unified hardware/software teleoperation platform. This paper presents an open-source teleoperation framework for MIRS to make research in surgical training, skill evaluation, and autonomy more accessible. The presented Accessible Surgical-robotics Platform for REsearch in Surgical Training, Skill Evaluation and Auton- omy (ASPIRE) consists of (1) a KUKA LBR iiwa7/UR5e, (2) a gimbal-free, 4 Degrees of Freedom (DOF) Low-Cost Master (LCM) device, (3) a hardware/software interface for control of existing surgical instruments from Intuitive Surgical’s da Vinci system and, (4) an open source software stack for integrating aforementioned components into a teleoperated surgical robotics system. The presented system transparently enforces the Remote Center of Motion (RCM) constraint in real-time while providing intuitive teleoperation capabilities. Teleoperation capabilities of the presented system were evaluated using Touch Haptic Device (THD) and LCM, showing a control loop rate of 120 Hz and following error for THD is 1.97%, 4.87%, and 3.615% over a distance of 80 mm in the x, y, and 65mm in z directions at a tool manipulation speed of 30mm/s or less, respectively following error for LCM is 4.5%, 2.03%, and 12.04%. Furthermore, system capabilities are demonstrated with peg transfer tasks, demonstrating the potential of this system towards democratizing surgical robotics research.
